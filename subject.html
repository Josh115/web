Bees are crucial to our society because of their ability to produce honey, and more importantly, the role they play in pollination.
Bees, as the biggest pollinator on the planet, pollinate billions of plants every year, including one-third of the food crops that we grow.
This pollination helps grow plants like trees, berries, nuts, and fruits which can serve as food sources and habitats for various other animals
For example, the pollination of clover and alfalfa, which are fed to cattle, help with the meat and diary industries.
Their honey and beeswax are used in many commercial products.
Bees' Importance to the Environment

When bees land on flowers to collect pollen, they 'collect' the pollen from the flower through the hairs on their legs
These hairs make use of electrostatic forces in order to attract the pollen to them. This and a bee's habit of collecting from
similar flowers allow for efficient pollination. Pollen on the bee hairs get moved from one flower to another,
producing seeds.
Bees and Pollination

Until the past decade, bee populations have been declining from pesticides, habitat destruction, global warming and parasites.
Bee populations have gone from 6 million hives in the 1940s to 2.4 million hives in 2008, creating a decrease in pollination levels
and crop production. Many pollination-limited crops like apples and cherries could be produced much faster with more bees.
